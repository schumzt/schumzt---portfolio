## 5. Stability Conditions

Identity Vector Integration (IVI) maintains stability only when its internal update processes follow a set of quantitative and structural constraints. Stability refers to the systemâ€™s ability to preserve coherent, continuous identity vectors across time while resisting drift, collapse, or oscillatory divergence.

Stability is defined as maintaining the identity deviation:

\[
D(t) = \| I(t) - I(t-1) \|
\]

within a bounded threshold:

\[
D(t) < \delta_{\text{max}}
\]

---

### 5.1 Activation Variance Constraint (AVC)

Identity becomes unstable when activation variance exceeds a manageable limit.

Let:

\[
A(t) = \text{activation level at time } t
\]

Variance is computed as:

\[
\text{Var}(A) = \mathbb{E}\big[(A(t) - \mu_A)^2\big]
\]

#### 1. Stability Requirement  
- Variance must remain below the upper bound:
\[
\text{Var}(A) < \sigma_{\text{max}}^2
\]

#### 2. Interpretation  
- Excessive variance implies emotional or contextual over-amplification.  
- Prevents runaway reactions triggered by transient noise.

---

### 5.2 Slow-Memory Decay Requirement

Long-term memory weights must decay gradually to preserve identity continuity.

Let:

\[
M(t) = \text{long-term memory strength}
\]

Decay constraint:

\[
\frac{dM}{dt} > -\lambda_{\text{min}}
\]

#### 1. Interpretation  
- If memory decays too quickly, identity continuity collapses.  
- Prevents fragmentation or context-dependent identity resets.

---

### 5.3 Boundary Saturation Limit

Identity vectors must operate within representational bounds.

For an identity vector \( I \in \mathbb{R}^n \):

\[
\| I(t) \| < B_{\text{max}}
\]

#### 1. Interpretation  
- Exceeding this threshold implies cognitive overload or extreme emotional states.  
- Prevents transitions into pathological attractors.

---

### 5.4 Noise-Resilience Condition

Let external disturbance be represented by noise \( \eta(t) \).

Stability requires:

\[
\| \eta(t) \| < \epsilon_{\text{max}}
\]

#### 1. Interpretation  
- Prevents hypersensitivity to minor environmental fluctuations.  
- Ensures robustness across unpredictable contexts.

---

### 5.5 Minimum Coherence Alignment

IVI stability requires coherence between identity sub-vectors.

Let \( I_k(t) \) and \( I_j(t) \) be sub-components.

\[
\text{sim}(I_k(t), I_j(t)) > \theta_{\text{min}}
\]

#### 1. Interpretation  
- Prevents contradictory self-representations.  
- Maintains global identity consistency across contexts.

---

### 5.6 Global Stability Criterion

The identity system is globally stable only when **all** constraints hold:

\[
\begin{aligned}
D(t) &< \delta_{\text{max}} \\
\text{Var}(A) &< \sigma_{\text{max}}^2 \\
\frac{dM}{dt} &> -\lambda_{\text{min}} \\
\| I(t) \| &< B_{\text{max}} \\
\| \eta(t) \| &< \epsilon_{\text{max}} \\
\text{sim}(I_k, I_j) &> \theta_{\text{min}}
\end{aligned}
\]

---

### 5.7 Summary

Stability in IVI is a regulated equilibrium state in which:

- identity deviation remains controlled  
- noise influence stays within tolerable bounds  
- sub-component similarity is preserved  
- long-term memory maintains continuity  
- representational magnitude remains within safe limits  

Stable IVI enables the identity system to integrate new experiences while maintaining coherence, continuity, and adaptability.
